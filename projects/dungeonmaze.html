What Is It?

Maze Game drops you into a dark dungeon maze with one goal: escape alive. You start at the top of the maze with 100 HP and a basic attack. Somewhere inside is a key — and somewhere else is the exit. Between you and freedom are four monsters, six mystery potions, and a layout that rewards exploration.

Every monster encounter is a choice: fight and earn points, or find another path. Potions are a gamble — they could restore your health, boost your attack power, or leave you poisoned and weakened right when you can't afford it. The stronger the monster you take down, the more points land on your score. Beat the boss guarding the exit for maximum reward.

How to Play

Find the Key

Hunt through the maze to locate the hidden key. You cannot leave without it.

Fight or Flee

Move into a monster to engage it in combat. Press Space to attack — but it hits back.

Risk the Potions

Walk over a potion to collect it. Effect is instant — helpful or harmful, no warning.

Reach the Exit

Find the glowing yellow exit square. With the key in hand, walk in — and you escape.

The Enemies

Weak — 20 pts
Low HP (15–25), low damage (3–6). Good for warming up and banking early points.
Normal — 40 pts
Balanced threat (25–35 HP, 5–8 dmg). You'll win, but you'll feel it.
Strong — 60 pts
Serious HP (35–50) and heavy hits (8–12). Guards the exit. Come prepared.
Boss — 100 pts
Maximum HP (50–70), brutal damage (10–15). The biggest score — if you survive.

The Potions

Six potions are placed randomly across the maze each game. Walk over one and the effect applies immediately — there is no preview. Two help you, two hurt you.

  • Health Potion — Restores 40 HP instantly. A lifesaver mid-combat.
  • Strength Potion — Boosts your attack by +5 for your next 5 hits.
  • Poison — Deals 20 damage to you immediately. Don't walk into this twice.
  • Weakness — Reduces your attack by 5 for your next 2 hits. Terrible timing before a boss.

What I Learned

Building a real-time game loop taught me a lot about how software has to manage multiple things at once — player input, enemy state, collision detection, UI rendering — all without slowing the game down. Structuring each element as its own class (Player, Monster, Potion) kept the code readable and made it much easier to add features without breaking what already worked.

Working with Pygame's event system forced me to think carefully about game state — when is the player allowed to move, when are they locked into combat, what triggers a win or a loss. These state management principles apply far beyond games, into any software that needs to respond to user actions in a defined, predictable way.
